Field methodology · Graphene photonics

The Ruler
Before the Material

Standardizing graphene as a photonic device layer runs on two coupled tracks — a material tier that already exists, and a photonic-integration tier that doesn't. This revision changes the proposal's nature: the measurand turns out to be already standardized, at the wrong frequency. The missing tier is an extension, not an invention.

Who this is for

  • Standards developers in IEC TC 113 / ISO TC 229 / IEC TC 86 scoping a device-tier spec
  • 2D-material suppliers who need a qualification target buyers will trust
  • Photonic foundries deciding what to demand on an incoming-material certificate
  • National metrology institutes building the reference methods underneath it

00 — The inversion

Standardize the measurement first.

The instinctive order is: make reproducible graphene, then write its spec. That stalls forever, because the thing blocking a spec is the variance — and you cannot reduce variance you cannot measure the same way twice. So put metrology first. Agreed, traceable methods are what render irreproducibility legible, discipline a process toward a target, and make any eventual threshold mean the same number in two labs.

Two honest qualifications, sharpened. "Before" applies to the measurement method, not the whole programme: method design genuinely can and should lead. But the step that validates the method — a multi-lab round-robin — needs a stable artifact to circulate, and that co-evolves with the material. So the honest form of the thesis is narrower than the slogan: the measurement standard leads the specification by roughly one development cycle; it does not run ahead of the material entirely. And metrology never replaces process control — in early-stage 2D integration the variance is frequently process-induced (transfer, interfaces, contamination); measurement does not fix that, it makes the deficiency quantitatively actionable. Metrology and process are co-equal here, not sequential.

01 — Two coupled tracks

A material tier and a photonic-integration tier, joined by an interface.

Standardization here is not one effort but two corresponding tiers with a defined handoff. Naming the handoff is the whole game.

Track A — Graphene as a material largely exists

Actor: the supplier · Output: a material certificate

  • Material-intrinsic key control characteristics: layer count, defect density, sheet resistance, mobility, doping, coverage — and, since 2021, a complex sheet conductivity at terahertz frequencies.
  • Already substantially covered by IEC TC 113 (62607-6-x) and ISO TC 229 (21356, 9651, 23359).
↓ The electronic–photonic material passport ↓
A passes the intrinsic matrix (layers, μ, doping, THz σs) · the transfer step is a mandatory declared field (wet/dry, polymer, anneal) · B passes back the processing delta (ΔEF from encapsulation, contact resistance, trap density)

Track B — Graphene in the photonic stack the gap

Actor: the foundry · Output: a device-integration certificate

  • Stack- and geometry-dependent optical performance: complex sheet optical conductivity at the operating wavelength, Γ-normalized loss and modulation, derived device KPIs.
  • No standard yet covers the interband optical response at 1550 nm, on the integrated stack.

The tiers are coupled, not independent. Track B references Track A — the Fermi level that governs the optical response is set by the doping and mobility Track A measures — and Track B adds the integration descriptors. The hoped-for payoff is attribution: hold both certificates and decompose a bad device into bad material versus bad integration. That is the interface's reason to exist — but it is not yet operational, and the brief should say so plainly. Transfer shifts doping by an unknown amount, so an off-spec optical loss could be bad pre-transfer material, an over-aggressive transfer, or a borderline film pushed over the edge — and today nothing tells them apart, because there is no standardized, post-transfer, in-situ measurement of EF on the finished stack. Raman estimates it but is non-unique under strain; on-device contacts perturb what they measure. So the passport is the scaffold that would enable attribution once post-transfer doping metrology exists — not a solution to attribution today. That missing measurement is itself one of the open problems this work points at. Note also where transfer lives: the single largest source of irreproducibility (tears, wrinkles, residue, wet-transfer doping), it is neither a Track A characteristic nor a Track B measurand, so it belongs at the interface as a mandatory declared field — because σs is not independent of it.

02 — What already exists (Track A)

The material tier is well underway — including this brief's own measurand, at the wrong frequency.

This is a correction worth stating plainly, and rev. 5 sharpens it further: the material and electrical tier is not a blank slate, and one of its published methods already extracts the very object Track B is built around. The gap is narrower and sharper than "graphene is unstandardized" suggests:

  • σ-tierComplex sheet conductivity — at terahertz. IEC TS 62607-6-10:2021 standardizes sheet conductance by terahertz time-domain spectroscopy: the complex THz transmission function yields the frequency-dependent complex sheet conductivity, with carrier density and mobility maps extracted by Drude fitting over large areas. This is the intraband response of a film on a THz-transparent substrate — not the interband response at 1550 nm, and not on a waveguide — but it establishes σs extraction as a published measurand inside the very series Track B targets.
  • ElectronicMobility, sheet R, carriers. Field-effect mobility by the FET method (62607-6-27:2025); sheet resistance by van der Pauw and four-point probe (62607-6-7 / 6-8:2023); non-contact surface conductance by microwave resonant cavity (62607-6-4:2024); carrier mobility and sheet resistance by Hall measurement (62607-6-23:2025 — scope now verified against the IEC catalogue), with a Hall majority/mobility/density method (62607-6-37) in development.
  • StructuralLayers, defects, classification. Structural characterization of CVD graphene by optical microscopy, TEM and Raman (ISO/TS 21356-2); layer-count distribution by optical contrast (IEC TS 62607-6-24:2026, published June 2026 — a non-destructive, high-throughput method, and fresh evidence the series is still actively absorbing optical methods on the material tier); terminology for graphene and 2D materials (ISO/TS 80004-13); and the materials-classification tier underneath everything here — ISO/TS 9651:2025 for classification of graphene-related 2D materials and ISO/TS 23359:2025 for chemical characterization.
  • Powder/chemBulk forms. Volume resistivity, density, oxygen functional groups (62607-6-1, 6-35, 6-13) — the particle/powder world, adjacent to but distinct from device-grade films.

What remains conspicuously absent is the photonic-integration form of that measurand: nothing defines propagation loss or absorption at a telecom wavelength, the modal-overlap-normalized optical response, or the qualification of graphene as a waveguide-integrated layer. The waveguide-absorption methods that exist are research papers, not standards. So the gap is precise — and the existence of 6-10 changes the committee pitch from "create a measurand" to "extend a standardized one." The series has already accepted σs as a key control characteristic, with a published extraction model. The photonic tier carries it across two changes: from the intraband Drude regime at terahertz to the interband regime at 1550 nm, where Pauli blocking makes σs a steep function of EF; and from a bare film to the integrated stack, where the modal overlap Γ enters. A harder measurement — and an easier proposal.

One caution against overstating Track A, though: "the electrical tier pins the operating point" is premature, and it carries the same intrinsic-versus-extrinsic problem this brief charges against the optical tier. Sheet resistance is 1/(e·n·μ) — identical Rs can hide different doping and therefore different EF, so it does not fix the operating point on its own; you need carrier density separately. A Hall method exists (6-23:2025) and the Hall extension (6-37) is in development, but a Hall bar is a fabricated test structure measured beside the device, not the as-delivered film in the optical mode — so an operating point still does not travel with the wafer. And FET mobility (6-27) is extracted on a back-gated SiO2 structure whose scattering environment is not the photonic stack's, so it too is a device-specific number, not an intrinsic one. Track A is real and useful, but it does not yet hand Track B a clean, stack-relevant operating point.

03 — The photonic measurand

Material-intrinsic, geometry-decoupled: the complex sheet conductivity.

You cannot standardize "graphene's optical loss," because in a photonic layer that number belongs to the graphene plus the waveguide cross-section, the spacer to the mode, the cladding, and the polarization. Measure loss directly and a silicon-nitride lab and a silicon lab get different numbers for identical material. The portable measurand is therefore the complex sheet optical conductivity, σs(λ, EF, T) — its real part governing absorption, its imaginary part governing phase.

And this measurand now has standards-series precedent. IEC TS 62607-6-10 already extracts exactly this object — a complex sheet conductivity from a measured complex transmission function, through a stated model with a defined analysis chain. What it measures is the free-carrier (intraband, Drude) response in the terahertz band, on a bare or polymer-supported film. What the photonic tier needs is the interband response at 1550 nm, on the integrated stack, through a modal overlap. The physics that makes that extension non-trivial is the same physics that makes it necessary: interband absorption is "on" near charge neutrality and switches off once |EF| exceeds ℏω/2 ≈ 0.4 eV at 1550 nm. That is why the spec must treat σs as an explicit function of EF — and why a small doping drift swings the loss steeply near the band edge. A THz Drude fit cannot be extrapolated across two decades of frequency into a different absorption mechanism; the photonic tier inherits 6-10's structure (declared model, declared substrate, complex measurand, map-capable method), not its numbers.

Geometry is divided out through the confinement factor. Under perturbative thin-sheet approximations, modal attenuation scales — up to a geometry-dependent constant set by the field at the sheet, not neff alone — as:

α ∝ Γ · Re{σs} α = modal loss · Γ = mode–graphene overlap, defined by complex modal perturbation theory (the surface integral of the field at the sheet over total modal power), not a naive area overlap. Report α/Γ and, within its validity range, the material number ports across platforms (see below). Derived mappings: Re{σs} → insertion loss · Im{σs} → phase shift, drive voltage, and the loss–phase trade Im/Re that sets a phase modulator's figure of merit · EF → operating point · μ → conductivity broadening.

The portability has a validity range, and the brief should not oversell it. Extracting σs from α needs Γ, but Γ depends on the modal field, which depends on the permittivity, which includes graphene's own contribution — a self-consistent inverse problem. The perturbative form breaks the loop by computing Γ for the bare waveguide and assuming the sheet barely perturbs the mode. For weakly-perturbed dielectric modes that holds; for the slot, plasmonic and ultra-confined hybrid modes that make graphene modulators efficient, the perturbation is exactly the signal and the clean decoupling fails — those cases need self-consistent extraction. Worse, in high-index-contrast waveguides the field at a 0.34 nm monolayer is so steep that a nanometre of spacer-thickness variation shifts it measurably, so the extracted σs silently absorbs a process tolerance. The standard must therefore declare the spacer geometry tightly and restrict its clean single-pass form to weakly-perturbed dielectric modes. Γ-normalization narrows the geometry dependence; it does not abolish it.

Two parameters are intrinsic and normalized (σs, reported per unit Γ); others are extrinsic (the stack and geometry) and must be declared, not buried. The device performance these map to — electro-absorption and electro-refraction modulation, the electro-optic index change exceeding 10−3, the wafer-scale targets — is the one set out in the Graphene Flagship roadmap (Romagnoli, Sorianello, Koppens … Ferrari, Nature Reviews Materials, 2018); Track B normalizes to those requirements rather than inventing its own.

04 — The reference test vehicle

The missing artifact is a standardized photonic test structure.

The FET-mobility standard works because it specifies a FET test structure to extract a material parameter. The photonic analogue is a standardized waveguide-plus-graphene vehicle — with a defined extraction (cut-back over varying graphene-covered lengths, or a Mach–Zehnder / ring with and without the sheet) to recover σs through a specified overlap model. This is the hardest part of the program, and the political objection is real: no foundry wants to adopt a competitor's waveguide geometry. The Γ-normalization eases that — within its validity range, a foundry may use its own cross-section as the vehicle, provided Γ is extracted rigorously and the stack geometry is declared, because the reported σs is then largely geometry-decoupled. "Largely," not "by construction": as Section 03 notes, the decoupling is only as clean as the weak-perturbation assumption and your nanometre-scale knowledge of the field at the sheet. The standard fixes the extraction, not the layout — but the extraction's accuracy is itself a declared, bounded quantity, not a free lunch.

For implementability the method must pin three things. The extraction model: a stated surface-current boundary condition with σs and a defined overlap integral, with uncertainty propagation — not just "fit the loss." The optical power: capped, because graphene saturates and at high intensity you measure saturable absorption, not the linear response. And the doping reference: Raman is an operating-point estimator, not a traceable anchor — strain–doping decomposition in (ωG, ω2D) space is non-unique without added constraints and is not metrologically traceable on its own, so it must carry a stated laser power-density limit (to avoid photo-thermal peak shifts that mimic strain or doping) and be cross-checked against the electrical operating point, not trusted alone.

And the vehicle-with-statistics concept is no longer hypothetical. The imec–Graphenea wafer-scale integration of graphene electro-absorption modulators in a 300 mm pilot CMOS environment used exactly this pattern — a fixed device design measured by the hundreds per wafer, with performance shifts attributed to individual process steps. What that run lacked is the standardized, declared extraction that would make its numbers portable to a second line. Which is the point.

05 — Pinning the operating point (the interface)

Reference the electrical tier; do not re-measure it.

This is Track A→B in action, with the caveat Section 02 already raised. The optical response at 1550 nm is governed by EF, which is set by doping and gating — quantities the electrical KCCs partially constrain (sheet resistance via 6-7/6-8, field-effect mobility via 6-27, Hall-method mobility and sheet resistance via 6-23:2025, with the 6-37 Hall extension in development). So the photonic standard does not re-invent doping metrology; it references those KCCs to bound the bias condition, then measures σs on top. But "bound" is honest where "fix" would not be: a Hall bar is a structure beside the device, not the film in the mode, and no standardized post-transfer, in-situ EF measurement on the finished stack exists. The ingredient science is moving — spatial mapping of Fermi-level dynamics under electrolyte gating was demonstrated in 2026 (Ivanko, Jindra, Frank & Velický, JACS) — but electrolyte gating is a laboratory probe, not an on-stack method, so the operating point reaching the optical measurement remains constrained, not pinned. That is why the attribution payoff in Section 01 is a scaffold rather than a solved capability — the interface is correctly placed, but one of the measurements it depends on has not been built yet.

On traceability tiers: comb-referenced wavelength defines the accuracy ceiling for the optical axis, but it is not a requirement for a production line. Foundry systems may use tunable lasers with wavelength meters calibrated by transfer standards; the comb sits at the reference tier, inherited downstream.

06 — Reproducibility and stability

Round-robins ratify; a stability matrix qualifies.

A standard needs artifacts to calibrate against, and graphene fights back — it adsorbs contaminants and its doping drifts, so a naked reference sample changes between measurements. The answer is encapsulation (e.g. hBN) and controlled storage, or protocols robust to drift; graphene's quantum Hall effect already anchors a primary resistance standard, so the material can support metrology. Inter-laboratory round-robins — many labs, same samples, same protocol — then build the uncertainty budget that turns a method into a standard, and that budget should be framed in GUM terms: Type A and Type B components, an explicit repeatability-versus-reproducibility split, and a stated traceability chain for the optical and electrical axes. One honest dependency: the round-robin itself needs material stable enough to survive a multi-month circulation, and it is not yet settled that any encapsulation stack meets that bar — so this step is gated by encapsulation maturity even though the standard does not require reproducible production material.

The pipeline is not hypothetical: the NPL-led VAMAS Technical Working Area 41 ("Graphene and related 2D materials") runs exactly these inter-comparisons and feeds them into ISO/IEC — its ILCs underpin ISO/TS 21356-1/-2 and ISO/TS 23359, new work items such as ISO/AWI TS 23879 are in the queue, and a 2025 review in 2D Materials maps the working area's full pipeline and its open challenges, reference materials chief among them. The first Raman round-robin cut measurement uncertainty by up to a factor of fifteen — after participants found, strikingly, that identical samples returned widely divergent values across labs. But the right lesson is bounded. That result proves two things: the institutional vehicle works, and measurement variance can be tamed in principle. It does not prove the photonic case is tractable — Raman on a substrate is a point measurement with no fabrication, no transfer, no alignment, no cut-back structures and no modal-overlap fit, whereas the photonic extraction has all of those. VAMAS is the precedent to extend, not evidence that the harder measurement will yield the same way. Treat it as the credible pipeline and the proof-of-principle; the technical tractability is still to be earned.

Stability must be operationalized, not gestured at. Report drift over defined intervals (e.g. 24 h and 168 h) under a specified storage atmosphere — dry N2 versus ambient air gives wildly different doping drift, so "controlled T/RH" is not enough — and under electrical bias stress and telecom-wavelength illumination, distinguishing shelf life from operating life. That qualification matrix is the first thing a committee will ask for.

07 — Wafer-scale statistics

A wafer is a distribution with structure, not a number.

Report mean, variance and acceptance as the fraction of area meeting threshold — but summary statistics miss patterned non-uniformity, which is what foundries actually fight. Include spatial metrics: radial gradients, edge effects, and a correlation length. Two non-contact mapping methods already exist in the series — the microwave resonant cavity (6-4) and THz-TDS (6-10), the latter producing carrier-density and mobility maps over very large areas — and the optical map can ride partly on them, but only through a platform-specific calibration. For the microwave link the dependence is doping, mobility, frequency and substrate; for the THz link it is all of that plus an extrapolation from intraband to interband response, so the caveat is stronger, not weaker. Calibrate per stack and state it explicitly.

The statistics themselves are demonstrably obtainable. The imec–Graphenea 300 mm pilot-CMOS run reported a modulation depth of 50 ± 4 dB mm−1 across 400 measured devices, with electro-optical bandwidth ~15 GHz for 25 μm devices — a per-wafer device population large enough to do attribution against process variables. That is the dataset shape this section calls for, produced in a foundry. The missing half is the standardized σs extraction on the same population, which is what would make those numbers comparable to a second line's. And the "machine-readable passport" is itself a sub-project, not a footnote: a format with no agreed schema — units, required versus optional fields, how spatial maps are encoded, versioning, missing-data handling — is just structured ambiguity. Interoperability standards in the semiconductor world took years; this one should be scoped as its own work item rather than assumed.

08 — Failure modes

What breaks the spec — and therefore why each measurand exists.

The physical failure modes below matter, but none is the most likely one. The most likely failure is quieter and more fundamental: that the standard specifies a measurement which, performed perfectly, yields a number that does not predict device performance. That happens if the test vehicle's overlap differs too much from real devices, if the standardized operating point doesn't match the device's, if the measurement runs at low power while devices operate near saturation, or if the film degrades in later processing the passport never saw. The brief assumes a correctly-measured σs predicts performance — and that is a modeling claim, not an empirical one. The imec wafer statistics are the first dataset with the right shape to test it — device KPIs by the hundreds against process variables — but a KPI-versus-process correlation is not yet a σs-versus-KPI validation. Validating it (Section 10, step 04) is the load-bearing experiment, not a formality. With that named, the narrower physical traps:

  • OpticalSaturable absorption · photo-induced doping. High intensity drives graphene out of the linear regime and can shift its doping under illumination — both corrupt σs unless power is bounded.
  • DriftAdsorbate hysteresis · doping instability. Ambient humidity p-dopes graphene and produces gate hysteresis, moving the operating point — the reason for encapsulation and the stability matrix.
  • InterfaceContamination · substrate charge trapping. Transfer residue raises loss; trapped charge in the dielectric shifts EF — both are integration faults, captured as process descriptors, not material faults.
  • GeometryPolarization · contact resistance. TE and TM overlap differ, so polarization must be stated; and in active devices, contact/interface impedance can dominate bandwidth, so it belongs in the report even though it is not optical.

09 — Committee pathway

Route it across boundaries — and budget for the friction.

The device-integration tier belongs with IEC TC 113, coordinated with ISO/TC 229 for the material tier it sits above and IEC TC 86 (fibre optics and photonics) for the optical-measurement methods. National metrology institutes — NPL, NIST, PTB — supply the traceability spine. Publish as a Technical Specification first, faster and provisional, then mature to an International Standard once round-robin data backs the numbers. The 6-10 precedent matters procedurally as well as technically: a proposal framed as the photonic-band extension of an existing 62607-6-x measurand, normalized to the Flagship roadmap's device targets, enters TC 113 as series continuity rather than new territory.

The adoption constituency has also changed since this brief's first revision, and it now carries a clock. CamGraPhIC's €211M Italian state-aid package was approved by the European Commission in April 2026, funding a graphene-photonics pilot manufacturing line near Milan scheduled to be operational in 2028 — and its first target application is publicly framed as the GPU-to-HBM optical link, against competitors such as Ayar Labs and Lightmatter, which makes the incoming-material-certificate demand concrete rather than notional; the Graphene Flagship's 2D pilot-line programme expanded its multi-project-wafer support in late 2025; Cambridge and Southampton joined the PIXEurope photonics pilot-line consortium; and Black Semiconductor acquired Applied Nanolayers to consolidate a graphene-photonics supply chain. Every one of those lines will need an incoming-material certificate, and none has a standard to point at. A TS that exists by the time the pilot lines qualify suppliers gets adopted by default; one that arrives later competes with whatever in-house spec each line improvised in the meantime.

The geography of that pull is asymmetric, and worth stating. The global supply of CVD graphene film is heavily Asian, and the region's metrology capability is embedded in the relevant machinery — VAMAS TWA 41 has been co-chaired from NIM China alongside NPL, and Japanese and Korean institutes are active in IEC TC 113 — so Track A is fully transnational. But no graphene-photonics foundry line or process design kit has been publicly disclosed in the region, so the clock-driven Track B demand is, for now, European. Two consequences follow. The standard must be globally applicable by design — which the extension framing delivers, since it builds on TC 113 measurands the Asian institutes already operate. And the first-mover window is real but not guaranteed: national standards bodies can move faster than international committees, and a domestic specification written first, in any jurisdiction, becomes the incumbent an international TS must then displace. The gap, in other words, is not a gap in Asian capability; it is the absence — so far, and so far as is publicly disclosed — of a commercial driver on the photonics side outside Europe.

Be realistic about the institutional cost: this measurand straddles materials, devices and optics, and cross-committee coordination is historically slow and fragmented. In practice it likely requires a joint working group or liaison structure — and that coordination, not the physics, may be the primary source of delay.

10 — The critical path

What gates what.

  1. 00Feasibility pre-studiesBefore anything: can two labs build the same bare waveguide to consistent loss, transfer the same graphene onto it to consistent loss, and converge on Γ across different mode solvers? If the transfer step fails here, the program is blocked by physics, not committee politics. This is the true gate.
  2. 01Scope the measurandsFix the envelope (1550 nm, platform class) and the vital few — explicitly as the interband, on-stack extension of the 6-10 measurand. Everything inherits this.
  3. 02Fix methods + traceabilityThe reference vehicle, extraction model, power bound, doping reference. The method design can lead; its validation (step 03) cannot.
  4. 03Reference materials + round-robinGated by 00 and 02 and by encapsulation stability. Produces the uncertainty budget that legitimises the standard.
  5. 04Parameter → KPI validationThe load-bearing experiment: does a correctly-measured σs actually predict device performance? The imec per-wafer device statistics show the dataset shape is producible in a foundry; the missing half is the standardized σs extraction on the same population. If the link fails, the measurand is wrong, however precise.
  6. 05Statistical / wafer frameworkWraps 02–04 into a population spec with spatial structure and a reporting passport (itself a schema sub-project).
  7. 06Committee ratificationTS first, then IS. Cross-committee liaison is the slowest, most human step.

The measurand was already standardized — at the wrong frequency, on the wrong substrate, for the wrong absorption mechanism. The unclaimed tier is the extension, and extensions are what committees know how to approve.

That is the strategic point rev. 5 adds to rev. 4's honesty. You do not wait for perfect graphene to design the measurement — and a designed, agreed measurement is the earliest scalable way to make everyone's irreproducibility legible and comparable. But the measurement cannot be validated, and the attribution payoff cannot be delivered, until the feasibility pre-studies pass and a post-transfer operating-point measurement exists. So this is a roadmap, not a finished method: it identifies a real gap, proposes a defensible direction anchored to an existing measurand, and is explicit about the several sub-problems — post-transfer doping metrology, self-consistent Γ extraction, transfer reproducibility, the σs-to-performance link — that remain open research, not settled craft. You still cannot cut to length without a ruler. This is the argument for extending the ruler the field already owns; it is not yet the ruler.
